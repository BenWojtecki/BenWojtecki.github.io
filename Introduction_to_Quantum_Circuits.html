Motivation

I have always been fascinated by quantum physics and its many applications. One of the most exciting recent developments is the rise of quantum computing, together with the new ideas it brings in algorithms, cryptography, and information processing. A particularly interesting aspect of quantum computing is the design of quantum gates used to control and manipulate quantum states. Because I enjoy sharing knowledge, I wanted to create a small tutorial introducing the standard quantum gates and showing how simple quantum circuits can be built and understood.

Introduction

Before studying quantum circuits and quantum gates, it is important to introduce the mathematical framework of quantum mechanics. Unlike classical computing, where information is stored using bits taking values in $\{0,1\}$, quantum computing relies on quantum states that can exist in superposition, meaning they can be in multiple configurations simultaneously. Quantum mechanics describes physical systems using vectors in a complex vector space called a Hilbert space. The state of a quantum system is represented by a unit vector in this space, and its evolution is described by unitary transformations. To manipulate these vectors, we use the Dirac notation, also known as the bra-ket notation. In this notation, a quantum state $|\psi\rangle$ is represented as a ket, and its dual vector is represented as a bra $\langle\psi|$. Consider a physical system that can be in $N$ distinct classical states, where a classical state corresponds to a configuration that can be observed upon measurement. A quantum state of such a system is a superposition of these basis states, written as: $$ |\psi\rangle = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \alpha_i |i\rangle $$ where $\alpha_i \in \mathbb{C}$ are called probability amplitudes associated with the basis state $|i\rangle$. Intuitively, the system is not in a single classical state, but in a coherent combination of all possible states. The states $|0\rangle, |1\rangle, \ldots, |N-1\rangle$ form an orthonormal basis of an $N$-dimensional Hilbert space. A quantum state $|\psi\rangle$ is a vector in this space, usually written as a $N$-dimensional column vector of its amplitudes. $$|\psi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha_0 \\ \alpha_1 \\ \vdots \\ \alpha_{N-1} \end{pmatrix}$$ Such a vector is called a ket. The corresponding bra vector is obtained by taking the conjugate transpose: $$ \langle\psi| = (\alpha_0^*, \alpha_1^*, \ldots, \alpha_{N-1}^*) $$ The reason for this terminology is that the inner product $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$ between two quantum states $|\phi\rangle$ and $|\psi\rangle$ correspond to the dot product between a bra and ket vector : $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle = \langle\phi| \cdot |\psi\rangle$. The inner product is a complex number that encodes the overlap between the two states. It is a fundamental quantity in quantum mechanics, as it determines the probability of transitioning from one state to another and the expectation values of observables.

Quantum bits

In classical computation, the unit of information is the bit, which can take values 0 or 1. In quantum computation, this unit is a quantum bit, or qubit, which is a superposition of $0$ and $1$. Consider a system with $2$ basis states, call them $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$. We identify these basis states with the two orthogonal vectors $|0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $|1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. A qubit is a quantum state of the form: $$|\psi\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle$$ According to the postulates of quantum mechanics, the state $|\psi\rangle$ must be normalized, meaning that the sum of the squares of the magnitudes of its amplitudes must be $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$. This normalization condition ensures that the total probability of all possible outcomes of a measurement is equal to 1. The state of a qubit can be visualized on the Bloch sphere, where the north pole corresponds to $|0\rangle$ and the south pole corresponds to $|1\rangle$. The superposition of these states allows quantum computers to perform certain computations more efficiently than classical computers, making them a powerful tool for tasks such as cryptography, optimization, and simulation of quantum systems. An important property of quantum states is that they can be entangled, meaning that the state of one qubit can be correlated with the state of another qubit in such a way that the state of the whole system cannot be described as a product of the states of the individual qubits. This phenomenon is a key resource for quantum computation and quantum communication, enabling protocols such as quantum teleportation and superdense coding. For instance, consider the $2$-qubit state that is in the following state: $$|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$$ Initially neither of the two qubits has a classical value $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$. However, if we measure the first qubit and observe, say, a $|0\rangle$, then the whole state collapses to $|00\rangle$. Thus observing the first qubit immediately fixes also the second, unobserved qubit to a classical value. Since the two qubits that make up the system may be far apart, this example illustrates some of the non-local effects that quantum systems can exhibit.

Basic quantum gates

In quantum computing, a quantum gate is a reversible linear transformation applied to a qubit. Unlike classical logic gates, which can be irreversible (e.g., AND, OR), quantum gates operate on probability amplitudes and preserve the normalization of quantum states. A quantum gate is represented by a unitary matrix, which is a complex square matrix that satisfies the condition $U^\dagger U = I$, where $U^\dagger$ is the conjugate transpose of $U$ and $I$ is the identity matrix.
